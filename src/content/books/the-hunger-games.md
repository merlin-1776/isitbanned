Suzanne Collins' 2008 novel is set in Panem, a dystopian nation built on the ruins of North America, where the authoritarian Capitol forces each of its 12 districts to send two teenagers to fight to the death in an annual televised spectacle. Katniss Everdeen, a 16-year-old from the impoverished District 12, volunteers to take her younger sister's place. The book launched a trilogy that sold over 100 million copies and a film franchise that earned nearly $3 billion worldwide.

In 2010, a parent in Goffstown, New Hampshire challenged the book at the school board, claiming it gave her 11-year-old daughter nightmares and could "numb children to violence." The ALA ranked it among the top 10 most challenged books in 2010, 2011, and 2014. Challenges have cited violence, "anti-ethnic" themes, "insensitivity," and the book being "unsuited to age group." Some challenges have also targeted the book's anti-government themes, which makes for a particularly ironic complaint about a novel whose entire premise is that governments suppress dissent.

## Why You Should Read This

Collins was channel-surfing one night and saw reality TV contestants competing for prizes cut against footage of the Iraq War. The two images blurred together. She wrote The Hunger Games.

Katniss Everdeen doesn't want to be a hero. She wants to keep her sister alive. Every "revolutionary" thing she does is an accident or an act of survival. That's what makes her believable: she's not giving speeches, she's just trying not to die on camera. The Capitol's real weapon isn't the Games themselves. It's the spectacle. The mandatory viewing. The forced celebrations. Collins understood that control doesn't require chains when you have screens.

The violence in this book is supposed to disturb you. That's the entire point Collins is making. When a parent says the book is "too violent for children," they're accidentally restating the book's thesis: that a society that makes children watch violence is a society that's already broken.
