Elana K. Arnold's Damsel opens like every fairy tale you've heard: a prince slays a dragon, rescues a maiden, and brings her home to be his queen. Then it peels back the story to reveal the rot underneath. The rescued girl, Ama, has no memory of her life before the dragon's lair. As she pieces together the truth about her kingdom's traditions, she discovers that the fairy tale cycle of rescue and marriage conceals something far more sinister. Arnold's novel is a pointed deconstruction of the damsel-in-distress trope and a meditation on how stories about women's passivity perpetuate real violence.

Damsel was challenged 16 times in the 2024-2025 PEN America data, with bans concentrated in Florida districts including Brevard, Broward, Hillsborough, Orange County, Palm Beach, and Pasco County. Challengers object to the book's sexual content and violence, both of which serve the novel's feminist argument about power and bodily autonomy. Arnold has become one of the most frequently banned authors in America, with multiple titles (Damsel, Red Hood, What Girls Are Made Of) appearing on removal lists. Her books tend to center girls and women confronting violence and reclaiming agency, subject matter that apparently makes certain school boards deeply uncomfortable.
