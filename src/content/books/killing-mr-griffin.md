Lois Duncan's 1978 thriller follows a group of high school students who decide to kidnap their strict, uncompromising English teacher, Mr. Griffin, as a prank. The plan is simple: scare him, make him beg, prove he's not so tough. But things go wrong fast. Mr. Griffin has a heart condition nobody knew about, and what started as a cruel joke becomes a cover-up, then something much worse. Duncan, who wrote over 50 novels for young readers, understood teenagers with an almost unsettling precision. The book works because every student's motivation is recognizable: the desire to belong, the thrill of rebellion, the slow horror of realizing you've gone too far.

Killing Mr. Griffin has been challenged 9 times, overwhelmingly in Florida, with additional challenges in Pennsylvania. Objectors cite violence, which is a fair description of what happens when a kidnapping goes sideways. But Duncan's novel isn't gratuitous. It's a morality tale about consequences, peer pressure, and how quickly a group dynamic can override individual conscience. The book has been a staple of middle school and high school reading lists for nearly 50 years precisely because it makes students uncomfortable in productive ways. Banning it removes one of the most effective YA thrillers ever written about the cost of going along with the crowd.
