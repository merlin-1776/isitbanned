Stephen King's It is a 1,138-page epic about seven kids in Derry, Maine, who confront a shape-shifting entity that feeds on children's fear, most often appearing as Pennywise the Dancing Clown. The novel alternates between the characters' childhood in 1958 and their return to Derry as adults in 1985, weaving together a story about the power of memory, the bonds of childhood friendship, and the particular terrors that only kids can see. King considered it his magnum opus, and he wasn't wrong. It's simultaneously a horror novel, a coming-of-age story, and a meditation on how trauma echoes across decades.

It has faced 12 challenges across Florida, Texas, and Tennessee, targeted for violence, sexual content, and profanity. The sexual content objection refers to a controversial scene near the end involving the child characters, which even fans debate. But reducing a 1,100-page novel to one scene is exactly the kind of context-free reading that book banners specialize in. King wrote It as a book about the loss of innocence and the courage it takes to face what frightens you. It's been terrifying readers for 40 years, and Pennywise remains the most recognizable horror villain in American fiction. Banning it from libraries just proves the book's central thesis: adults forget what it's like to be afraid.
