Jennifer Mathieu's debut novel tells the story of Alice Franklin, a high school girl whose reputation is destroyed after a party, through the voices of four classmates who each contributed to the rumor mill. One narrator is a popular girl protecting her own secrets. Another is a jock covering up something far worse. Each chapter peels back another layer of lies, revealing more about the narrators' insecurities than about Alice herself. It's a sharp, compact novel about slut-shaming, social cruelty, and the way small-town gossip can ruin a life before anyone bothers to check the facts.

The Truth About Alice has been challenged 11 times across Florida, Pennsylvania, North Dakota, South Carolina, and Utah, flagged for sexual content. The irony is thick: the book is specifically about how dangerous it is to reduce a person to their sexual reputation, and the challenges do exactly that to the book itself. Mathieu wrote it as a feminist response to the way communities punish young women for their sexuality (real or imagined) while excusing young men for the same behavior. It's required reading for anyone who's ever watched a rumor take on a life of its own.
