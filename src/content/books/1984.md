1984 imagines a world of perpetual war, omnipresent government surveillance, and the systematic destruction of truth. Winston Smith works at the Ministry of Truth, rewriting historical records to match the Party's ever-shifting version of reality. When he begins a forbidden love affair and secretly rebels against the regime, he discovers just how total the Party's control really is. Orwell's novel gave the English language "Big Brother," "thoughtcrime," "doublethink," and "Newspeak," concepts that have only become more relevant in the age of mass surveillance and political disinformation.

Paradoxically, 1984, one of the most famous literary warnings against censorship, has itself been repeatedly censored. The novel has been challenged from both the political left and right: some object to its sexual content and graphic torture scenes, while others have called it "pro-communist" or "anti-government." With over 160 total challenges spanning decades, it continues to be removed from school libraries in Florida, Texas, Iowa, Missouri, and Virginia. The irony of banning a book about the suppression of free thought has been noted by essentially everyone except the people doing the banning.
