Ellen Hopkins's Fallout is the conclusion of the Crank trilogy, but it shifts perspective from Kristina Snow to her three children, each raised in different circumstances after their mother's meth addiction shattered the family. Hunter lives with his grandparents and struggles with anger. Autumn was adopted and discovers her birth mother's identity. Summer lives with Kristina and her current boyfriend, witnessing the ongoing destruction of addiction firsthand. Hopkins traces the intergenerational damage of substance abuse through three distinct voices, showing how one person's choices ripple outward for decades.

Fallout was challenged 11 times in the 2024-2025 PEN America data, with bans in Colorado, Florida, and Tennessee school districts. The book completes a trilogy that has been among the most challenged in American libraries for nearly two decades. The objections cite drug content, sexual content, and language, the same reasons cited for Crank and Glass. Hopkins based the Crank trilogy on her own daughter's meth addiction, making the books some of the most autobiographically grounded YA fiction about substance abuse. Removing them from shelves takes away a resource that substance abuse counselors and educators have consistently recommended.
